Emergency Healthcare
in Thailand

What to do, where to go, and what to expect if you need medical care in Thailand. Written from a healthcare professional's perspective.

Emergency numbers in Thailand:
1669 — National EMS (ambulance)
191 — Police
1155 — Tourist Police (English-speaking)

The two-tier system: private vs public hospitals

Thailand has one of the strongest private hospital sectors in Southeast Asia. Private hospitals in Bangkok and major tourist cities offer internationally accredited care with English-speaking staff, modern equipment, and fast emergency processing. They also charge accordingly — costs comparable to or exceeding Western private healthcare without travel insurance.

Public hospitals offer lower costs but significantly longer waits and variable English capability. For non-emergency situations where cost matters, public hospitals are functional. For an emergency — especially a serious allergic reaction, cardiac event, or significant injury — go directly to a private hospital.

Best private hospitals by city

Bangkok

Bumrungrad International Hospital

Sukhumvit Soi 3, Bangkok. One of the most internationally accredited hospitals in Asia. Handles the highest volume of foreign patients. 24-hour emergency department. Most international insurance accepted directly.

Emergency: +66 2 667 1000

Bangkok Hospital (Bangkok)

Multiple locations across Bangkok and Thailand. The Bangkok Hospital chain has strong emergency infrastructure and branches in most major tourist destinations. Reliable choice outside Sukhumvit if Bumrungrad is not accessible.

Samitivej Hospital (Sukhumvit)

Good alternative for eastern Bangkok. Strong pediatric department if relevant. International patient center handles insurance paperwork.

Chiang Mai

Bangkok Hospital Chiang Mai

Part of the Bangkok Hospital chain. International emergency care, English-speaking staff. Located on Superhighway near the Night Bazaar area.

Chiang Mai Ram Hospital

Well-regarded private hospital with experienced international patient services.

Phuket

Bangkok Hospital Phuket

Main private referral hospital for Phuket. High volume of international patients. Strong emergency and trauma capability given the tourist population.

Phuket International Hospital

Reasonable alternative, particularly for Patong-area access.

Travel insurance — what you actually need

Thailand private hospital costs are not covered by most domestic health insurance plans. A serious emergency — multi-day ICU admission, surgical procedure, medical evacuation — can exceed $50,000 USD without insurance. This is not a theoretical scenario.

What to look for in a travel insurance policy for Thailand:

  • Emergency medical coverage minimum $100,000 USD per incident
  • Emergency evacuation included (covers transport if local care is inadequate)
  • Direct billing with major Thai hospitals, not just reimbursement
  • Pre-existing condition coverage if relevant
  • Coverage for adventure activities if you plan diving, motorbikes, etc.

For food allergy emergencies specifically

If you carry an EpiPen or adrenaline auto-injector: keep it with you at all times — not in a hotel safe, not in checked luggage. In hot weather, don't leave it in a car or bag in direct sunlight; the medication degrades above 25°C if exposed for extended periods.

After using an EpiPen, go immediately to a hospital. Epinephrine provides 15–30 minutes of effect; a second wave of anaphylaxis (biphasic response) can occur hours later. Thai emergency departments understand anaphylaxis treatment — this is not an area where you'll encounter knowledge gaps.

The Thai phrase to say at an emergency desk: แพ้อาหารรุนแรง (pae ahan run raeng) — severe food allergy.

Frequently asked questions

Will Thai hospitals treat me without insurance documents?

Yes. Private hospitals in Thailand will treat any patient presenting to emergency, and will sort billing afterward. You will be asked for a deposit or credit card for large treatments, but you won't be turned away from emergency care for not having immediate proof of insurance. Carry your insurance policy number and emergency contact number from your insurer.

Can I buy medication in Thailand without a prescription?

Thailand has a relatively accessible pharmacy system. Many medications that require prescriptions in Western countries — including certain antibiotics, antihistamines, and anti-diarrhea medications — are available over the counter at Thai pharmacies (ร้านขายยา). However, prescription antihistamines and EpiPens still require prescription or are unavailable over the counter. Don't assume you can replace a critical medication on arrival.

Is dengue fever a real risk in Thailand?

Yes. Dengue is endemic across Thailand and cases peak during the rainy season (May–October). Symptoms are fever, severe headache, muscle pain, and sometimes rash — beginning 4–14 days after an infected mosquito bite. There is no specific antiviral treatment; management is supportive. Any high fever after visiting Thailand warrants a dengue test. Private hospitals test routinely.

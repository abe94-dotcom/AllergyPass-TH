Why Thailand is different

Thai food is extraordinary. For allergy sufferers, it's also one of the riskiest cuisines in the world.

Street food stalls serve hundreds of dishes a day from a single wok. Sauces, pastes, and oils are added by habit — not recipe. Shrimp paste appears in curries you'd never expect it in. Peanut oil fries dishes that seem peanut-free. Fish sauce is the salt of Thai cooking. Even experienced travellers with allergies are caught off guard. The language barrier makes it nearly impossible to explain the severity of an anaphylactic reaction in the moment, at a busy market stall, with a language gap between you and the person cooking your food.

Common hidden risks

These ingredients are in Thai food — even when you can't see them.

🥜 Peanut oil

Used for frying across street food stalls. Often unlabelled and assumed to be safe by vendors.

🦐 Shrimp paste (กะปิ)

Base ingredient in many curries, dipping sauces, and stir-fry pastes. Present in dishes that seem entirely vegetarian.

🐟 Fish sauce (น้ำปลา)

The primary seasoning in Thai cooking. Used the way salt is used in Western food — in almost everything.

🍳 Cross-contamination

Shared woks, shared oil, shared utensils. Street kitchens rarely separate allergen preparation.

🌾 Soy sauce (ซีอิ๊ว)

Contains gluten and wheat. Used in stir-fries, marinades, and dipping sauces throughout Thai cuisine.

🦪 Oyster sauce (ซอสหอยนางรม)

A shellfish-derived sauce added to noodle dishes, stir-fries, and street food across Thailand.

What your phone says: "I am allergic to peanuts"

A physical card — large Thai text, organized by allergen, showing severity and hidden ingredients — can be handed directly to the chef. It communicates everything, instantly, without a conversation.

Common questions

FAQ

Can I eat street food in Thailand with a food allergy?

Yes, but you need to be cautious and proactive. Many dishes can be modified — but only if the vendor understands your allergy clearly. The biggest risks are hidden ingredients (shrimp paste, fish sauce, peanut oil) that are added by default. A written allergy card in Thai is far more effective than verbal communication, especially at busy street food markets like Chatuchak or night bazaars. Always confirm before eating, and carry antihistamines or an EpiPen if your allergy is severe.

How do I explain a peanut allergy in Thai?

The Thai phrase for peanut allergy is ฉันแพ้ถั่วลิสง (pronounced: chăn pâe tùa lí-sŏng). But a phrase alone often isn't enough — vendors may not realize that peanut oil (น้ำมันถั่วลิสง) or peanut sauce (ซอสถั่ว) also counts. AllergyCard TH generates a card that lists your allergy in large Thai text along with the hidden peanut-based ingredients to avoid, making it far clearer for kitchen staff.

What foods should I avoid in Thailand if I'm allergic to shellfish?

Beyond obvious seafood dishes, shellfish derivatives appear in: กะปิ (shrimp paste) used in most curries and stir-fry bases; ซอสหอยนางรม (oyster sauce) added to pad thai and stir-fries; น้ำปลา (fish sauce) used as the primary seasoning in Thai cooking; and เต้าเจี้ยว (fermented bean paste), which may be made with shellfish. These appear in dishes that look entirely safe on a menu.

Is pad thai safe for people with peanut allergies?

No — standard pad thai contains crushed peanuts and is often fried in peanut oil. You can ask for it without peanuts (ไม่ใส่ถั่ว), but cross-contamination from shared woks is still a real risk. If your allergy is anaphylactic, you should exercise significant caution. Use your allergy card to communicate the full severity to the chef, not just the ingredient to omit.

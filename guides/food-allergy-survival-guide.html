Thailand Food Allergy
Survival Guide

How to eat safely in Thailand when you have food allergies. What's actually in the food, how to communicate with staff, and which situations carry the most risk.

Quick answer: The four highest-risk hidden allergens in Thai food are fish sauce (น้ำปลา), shrimp paste (กะปิ), peanuts/peanut oil (ถั่วลิสง), and dried shrimp (กุ้งแห้ง). These appear in dishes that give no indication of containing seafood or nuts. A written card in Thai is the most reliable way to communicate your needs.

Why food allergies in Thailand are genuinely harder

Thailand has a complex culinary tradition built on layered sauces and pastes. The base flavors in most Thai dishes come from condiments — fish sauce, shrimp paste, oyster sauce — that are added during cooking and invisible in the final dish. This is not unique to street food. It applies equally to restaurant kitchens.

A dish described as "vegetable stir-fry" on an English-language menu may be cooked with oyster sauce as a standard ingredient. A clear soup may be seasoned with fish sauce as the base stock. This isn't carelessness — it's how Thai cooking works. The challenge is that the people cooking often don't consider these as "ingredients" in the way a Western kitchen would. They're seasoning, not additions.

The four hidden allergens to know

🐟
น้ำปลา (Nam Pla)
Fish Sauce
High risk

Present in the majority of Thai curries, stir-fries, soups, and dipping sauces. It's used as salt in Thai cooking. Dishes labeled vegetarian at Western-facing restaurants often still use fish sauce unless explicitly specified. At street stalls, assume it's present unless you've confirmed otherwise.

Thai to show: ไม่ใส่น้ำปลา (no fish sauce)

🦐
กะปิ (Kapi)
Shrimp Paste
High risk

A fermented shrimp paste used as a foundational ingredient in curry pastes, nam prik (chili dips), and as a base for som tam (papaya salad). It's cooked into the dish rather than added at the table, which means it's difficult to remove on request. Anyone with shellfish allergy needs to confirm this specifically.

Thai to show: ไม่ใส่กะปิ (no shrimp paste)

🥜
ถั่วลิสง (Thua Lisong)
Peanuts / Peanut Oil
High risk

Used as both a cooking fat and a garnish. Peanut oil is common in street food woks. Whole or crushed peanuts appear as a topping on pad thai, som tam, satay, and many noodle dishes. They're often added after plating, which makes cross-contamination a real concern even when the main dish doesn't contain them.

Thai to show: แพ้ถั่วลิสง (allergic to peanuts)

🦐
กุ้งแห้ง (Kung Haeng)
Dried Shrimp
Medium risk

Small dried shrimp used as a flavor component in som tam, fried rice, stir-fried morning glory, and many noodle dishes. They're sometimes visible and sometimes ground into the dish. People with shellfish allergies frequently overlook this one.

Thai to show: ไม่ใส่กุ้งแห้ง (no dried shrimp)

How to communicate a food allergy in Thailand

1. Use a written card, not verbal requests

This is the most important practical advice in this guide. Verbal requests in English are frequently misunderstood or misinterpreted — not because of bad intent, but because of the noise level in street markets, the language gap, and the unfamiliar vocabulary of allergy communication. A written card in Thai removes that ambiguity.

The card should name specific allergens in Thai, not just say "I'm allergic to shellfish." Staff at a stall may not connect "shellfish" to the dried shrimp they routinely add to fried rice. Naming กุ้งแห้ง specifically is more effective than naming a category.

2. Show the card before ordering

Hand the card to whoever is cooking, not just the person taking your order. In Thai food service — especially at street stalls — the person who takes the order and the person who cooks are often different people, and the information doesn't always pass between them reliably.

3. Carry your card on your phone, not just on paper

When to seek medical attention

Thailand has good private hospital infrastructure in Bangkok, Chiang Mai, Phuket, and other major tourist areas. If you carry an EpiPen, keep it accessible at all times — not in checked luggage. For a severe reaction, going directly to a private hospital emergency department is faster than waiting for an ambulance in most Thai cities.

Hospitals with international emergency departments include Bumrungrad International (Bangkok), Bangkok Hospital (multiple locations), and Samitivej. Most private hospitals can process travel insurance claims directly.

Emergency number for ambulance in Thailand: 1669.

Frequently asked questions

Is Thai food safe for someone with a peanut allergy?

Thai food can be eaten safely with a peanut allergy, but it requires active communication and caution. Peanuts are widespread in Thai cooking and cross-contamination is common in street food environments where peanuts are used freely. A written allergy card that specifically names peanuts in Thai is essential. Avoid dishes where peanuts are a core component (pad thai, satay) unless the kitchen has confirmed they can prepare it safely.

Can I eat vegetarian food in Thailand if I have a shellfish allergy?

Not automatically. Thai vegetarian food (เจ, je) avoids meat but fish sauce and shrimp paste are sometimes included even in dishes labeled vegetarian at non-specialist restaurants. Look for restaurants with a yellow je flag, which indicates stricter vegetarian preparation. Even then, confirm specifically that shrimp paste is not being used.

What does an allergic reaction look like in Thailand — what should I do?

Symptoms range from hives and itching to vomiting, swelling, and anaphylaxis. If you have a known severe allergy, carry your emergency medication and know the Thai for "I need help" (ช่วยด้วย, chuai duai). For anaphylaxis or difficulty breathing, get to a private hospital emergency department immediately. Call 1669 for ambulance. Tell staff at the hospital what you ate — a photo of the dish helps.

Are there allergy-friendly restaurants in Thailand?

Yes, particularly in Bangkok, Chiang Mai, and Phuket. Some restaurants specifically cater to Western allergy expectations. However, even in allergy-aware restaurants, the underlying Thai cuisine uses these ingredients as base seasonings, so the kitchen needs to prepare dishes differently — not just omit a topping. The safest approach is always a specific written request rather than assuming the kitchen has adapted a dish for you.

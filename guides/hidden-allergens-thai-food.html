Hidden Allergens
in Thai Food

Thai cuisine's flavor base comes from condiments and pastes that rarely appear on menus but are present in most dishes. This is where most allergic reactions in Thailand originate.

Direct answer: The four most common hidden allergens in Thai food are fish sauce (น้ำปลา), shrimp paste (กะปิ), peanuts and peanut oil (ถั่วลิสง), and dried shrimp (กุ้งแห้ง). These appear in dishes that give no indication of containing seafood or nuts — including many dishes marketed as vegetarian.

Why Thai food is unusually high-risk for hidden allergens

Thai cuisine is built on layered flavors that originate in sauces and pastes added during cooking. Fish sauce functions as salt. Shrimp paste is part of the base for curry pastes. Peanut oil is a common cooking fat. None of these appear on a menu description, and Thai kitchen staff often don't think of them as "ingredients to mention" — they're as fundamental as salt and oil in a Western kitchen.

The result: a dish described as "mixed vegetables with tofu" can be stir-fried in peanut oil, seasoned with fish sauce, and served with a shrimp paste-based dipping sauce. None of that is visible from the description, and the server may not know to flag it.

Fish sauce — น้ำปลา (nam pla)

The single most common hidden allergen in Thai food. Fish sauce is used where salt would be used in Western cooking — it seasons dishes during cooking and is often added at the table as a condiment.

It appears in: pad thai, fried rice, tom yum soup, most Thai curries, papaya salad, stir-fried vegetables, and almost any dipping sauce. Even dishes at restaurants marketing themselves as vegetarian or health-focused frequently contain fish sauce as a background flavoring unless explicitly prepared otherwise.

People with fish allergy: fish sauce is made from fermented fish and contains fish proteins. It is a genuine allergen risk, not just a flavor concern.

Shrimp paste — กะปิ (kapi)

Fermented shrimp paste used as a foundational ingredient in curry pastes (red, green, massaman, panang), nam prik (chili dipping sauces), and som tam. Unlike fish sauce which is added during cooking, shrimp paste is cooked into the base of the dish — it cannot be removed on request.

The important distinction: a kitchen that prepares a curry from premade paste cannot remove the shrimp paste from that dish. The only solution is a dish prepared from scratch with paste that doesn't contain kapi, which requires specific communication well in advance.

People with shellfish allergy should treat shrimp paste as a confirmed exposure risk in any Thai curry unless the restaurant has specifically confirmed their curry paste is shrimp-free.

Peanuts and peanut oil — ถั่วลิสง (thua lisong)

Used as both a cooking fat and a garnish. Peanut oil is common in street food cooking where high-heat wok cooking is standard. Whole or crushed peanuts are added as a garnish to pad thai, som tam, and many noodle dishes — often sprinkled on after the dish is plated, meaning cross-contamination is almost impossible to avoid even when the main dish is prepared without peanuts.

The additional risk: peanut-heavy dishes (satay, pad thai, som tam) are cooked in the same woks and at the same stations as everything else in a street food environment. Cross-contamination is essentially a certainty at street stalls for anyone with anaphylactic peanut allergy.

Dried shrimp — กุ้งแห้ง (kung haeng)

Small dried shrimp used as a flavor component rather than a main ingredient. They appear in som tam, fried rice, morning glory stir-fry, and many noodle dishes. They're sometimes visible and sometimes ground into the dish to the point of invisibility.

This is the allergen most frequently overlooked by people with shellfish allergy who focus on visible shrimp and crabs but miss the dried shrimp used as a flavoring. A person who successfully avoids whole shrimp in dishes may still be exposed to dried shrimp without realizing it.

Other allergens worth knowing

Oyster sauce (ซอสหอยนางรม): Used as a stir-fry seasoning — present in many vegetable and meat dishes. Contains shellfish. Also frequently used as a substitute for fish sauce in some preparations.

Sesame oil (น้ำมันงา): Used as a finishing flavor in some dishes. Less universally present than fish sauce but worth noting for sesame allergy.

Coconut milk (กะทิ): Present in most Thai curries and some desserts. Important for those with tree nut allergy who also react to coconut.

Frequently asked questions

What hidden allergens are common in Thai food?

The four most common hidden allergens in Thai food are fish sauce (น้ำปลา), shrimp paste (กะปิ), peanuts/peanut oil (ถั่วลิสง), and dried shrimp (กุ้งแห้ง). These appear in the vast majority of Thai dishes without being listed on the menu.

Is Thai vegetarian food safe for someone with a shellfish allergy?

Not automatically. Standard Thai vegetarian food (มังสวิรัติ) avoids meat but fish sauce and shrimp paste are sometimes included. Je (เจ) food — the stricter Buddhist vegetarian style, identified by yellow flags — avoids all animal products including fish sauce and shrimp paste, making it a safer choice. Even then, confirm dried shrimp specifically.

Does pad thai contain peanuts?

Yes, typically. Peanuts are a standard component of pad thai — both in the wok and as a garnish added after plating. Even if you ask for "no peanuts," cross-contamination from a shared cooking surface is highly likely at street stalls. For a confirmed peanut allergy, pad thai at street stalls carries significant risk.

How do I tell a Thai restaurant I'm allergic to fish sauce?

In Thai: ไม่ใส่น้ำปลา (mai sai nam pla) — no fish sauce. A written card showing this phrase alongside your other allergens in Thai is more reliable than saying it verbally. AllergyPass TH generates this card with specific phrases for fish sauce and other hidden ingredients.
